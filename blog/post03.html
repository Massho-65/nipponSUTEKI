A Living Sanctuary in the Heart of Tokyo

by Kiri

Hello. I’m so glad you’re here today. Please come in, take a deep breath, and make yourself comfortable.

Imagine us standing together at the edge of a vast, emerald-green forest. Can you hear it? The trains, the crowds, the neon energy of Tokyo slowly fade away, replaced by the gentle rustling of leaves and the soft crunch of gravel beneath our feet.

Today, I’d like to introduce you to a living masterpiece — not painted on canvas, but carved from ancient wood and embraced by nature itself. Let us visit one of Japan’s most moving sanctuaries: Meiji Shrine.

Urban Sanctuary

If Tokyo is a vibrant modern symphony, Meiji Shrine is the breathtaking silence between the notes.

Located in the very heart of the city — between the fashionable streets of Harajuku and the skyscrapers of Shinjuku — this shrine feels like another world. The moment you pass beneath the enormous wooden torii gate, the atmosphere shifts completely.

Sunlight filters through more than 100,000 trees, casting a gentle dance of light and shadow along the path. It’s almost impossible to believe that this entire forest was planted by hand just over a century ago.

Created as an offering of devotion to Emperor Meiji and Empress Shoken, this forest is a quiet testament to faith, patience, and love. Doesn’t that warm your heart?

Architecture: A Masterpiece of Nature

At the heart of the shrine stands the Honden, the sacred main hall. This is not merely a building — it is an expression of harmony between human craftsmanship and the natural world.

The structure is made entirely of Japanese cypress, known as Hinoki. This precious wood is revered in traditional architecture for its soft golden color and its calming, pure fragrance.

Look up at the roof, and you may notice something unusual. It is crafted using an ancient technique called Kayabuki, where thick layers of dried grass form a gently curved silhouette.

These heavy thatched roofs resemble the wings of a resting bird — modest, grounded, yet deeply dignified. Unlike cold stone or glass towers, this structure feels as though it was born directly from the earth.

About the Craftsmanship
Every few decades, skilled artisans carefully replace these roofs, preserving ancient techniques and passing them on to future generations. This quiet cycle of renewal ensures that the shrine continues to live and breathe.

The Beauty of the New Year

While Meiji Shrine remains calm throughout the year, the first days of January reveal a very special side of it. This is Hatsumode — the first shrine visit of the new year.

Millions of people gather here, wrapped in winter coats or dressed in beautiful traditional kimonos. They offer prayers for health, happiness, and fresh beginnings.

Coins gently fall, hands clap softly to greet the spirits, and hopes are written on small wooden plaques called ema. Despite the immense crowds, the air is filled with quiet optimism and renewal.

It’s a gentle reminder that no matter how fast the world moves, we all long for moments of stillness — to connect with nature, the divine, and our own hearts.

Kiri

When I visit Meiji Shrine, I always feel as if my thoughts naturally slow down.

Strength doesn’t always need to be loud or impressive. Sometimes, it’s found in quiet places — where nature and people gently coexist.

A Moment of Meditation

When you stand before Meiji Shrine, you are not simply visiting a sightseeing spot. You are gazing into the Japanese spirit, which values harmony above all else.

Trees, wooden halls, thatched roofs, and people exist together in a balanced composition. This place teaches us that true strength can be quiet, gentle, and deeply rooted.

Thank you for joining me on this small journey through a living landscape painting. Sharing stories like this is always a joy — especially with those who cherish life’s quiet moments.
